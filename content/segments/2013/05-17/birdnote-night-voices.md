## Transcript

CURWOOD: The advent of spring brings with it the arrival of migrating birds - and fills our skies and gardens with song. And as Mary McCann reminds us in today's BirdNote®, the cheerful warbling is not confined to daylight.

{% cue text="CRICKETS CHIRPING" %}

{% cue text="BUFF COLLARED NIGHTJAR CALLS" %}

MCCANN: As darkness descends on a May evening, the voices of many birds go quiet.  
But for some birds, especially those known as nightjars, the music is just beginning. In the moonlit shadows of an Eastern hardwood forest, an Eastern Whip-poor-will shouts out its name.

{% cue text="WHIP-POOR-WILL CALL, REPEATED" %}

MCCANN: The same evening, in a Southeastern woodland, we hear the loud calls of a Chuck-will’s-widow.

![Chuck –will’s widow (Glen Peterson)](https://loe.org/content/2013-05-17/Chuck-will's-widow---Photo-by-Glen-K.jpg)

{% cue text="CHUCK-WILL’S-WIDOW CALLS, REPEATED" %}

![Common Poorwill (Ken-ichi Ueda)](https://loe.org/content/2013-05-17/Common-Poorwill-by-Ken-ichi-Ueda.jpg)

MCCANN: West of the Rockies, the voice of a Common Poorwill echoes across a canyon.

{% cue text="COMMON POORWILL CALLS, REPEATED" %}

![Common Pauraque (Jason Forbes)](https://loe.org/content/2013-05-17/Common-Pauraque---Jason-Forbes_brewsterslinnet.jpg)
